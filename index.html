Fun Facts About the Universe

Bite-sized wonders from our cosmos

A spoonful of a neutron star weighs about 6 billion tons.
There are more trees on Earth than stars in the Milky Way.
A day on Venus is longer than its year.
99% of our solar system’s mass is in the Sun.
The largest known star is over 1,700 times wider than the Sun.
Light from the Sun takes about 8 minutes and 20 seconds to reach Earth.
Saturn is less dense than water — it would float in a gigantic bathtub.
Olympus Mons on Mars is the tallest volcano in the solar system, ~22 km high.
Some neutron stars (pulsars) spin hundreds of times per second.
The observable universe is about 93 billion light-years across.
Jupiter’s Great Red Spot is a storm that’s raged for at least 300 years.
The Boomerang Nebula is the coldest known place in the universe (~1 K).
Mercury’s day lasts ~59 Earth days, while its year is ~88 Earth days.
There may be more stars in the universe than grains of sand on all Earth’s beaches.

Core Astronomical Domains

Survey six foundational classes of astronomical phenomena — Planets, Stars, Galaxies, Nebulae, Black Holes, and Comets — that together outline the structure and dynamics of our universe.

PLANETS

Rocky and gaseous worlds orbiting stars, including our own Earth and the eight planets of our solar system.

STARS

Massive balls of plasma held together by gravity, like our Sun, that shine through nuclear fusion.

GALAXIES

Vast systems of stars, gas, and dust bound by gravity, like our Milky Way containing billions of stars.

NEBULAE

Giant clouds of gas and dust in space where new stars are born, creating spectacular cosmic nurseries.

BLACK HOLES

Regions of spacetime with such intense gravity that nothing, not even light, can escape their pull.

COMETS

Icy bodies that orbit the Sun, developing spectacular tails when they approach and heat up.
