Galaxies

Quick facts about some of the most famous galaxies.

Milky Way Andromeda (M31) Triangulum (M33) Whirlpool (M51) Sombrero (M104)

Milky Way

Our Home • Barred Spiral
Our galaxy — a vast barred spiral with a supermassive black hole at its center. The Sun orbits once every ~230 million years.
  • Diameter ~100,000 light‑years; contains hundreds of billions of stars.
  • We live in the Orion Arm, a minor spiral arm.

Andromeda (M31)

Nearest Large Galaxy • Future Merger
The closest big neighbor to the Milky Way. On track to merge with us in ~4–5 billion years.
  • About 2.5 million light‑years away — visible to the naked eye in dark skies.
  • Larger than the Milky Way with a bright central bulge.

Triangulum (M33)

Local Group • Star‑Forming
A smaller spiral galaxy in our Local Group. Popular with astrophotographers for its detailed star‑forming regions.
  • Roughly 3 million light‑years away.
  • Home to the bright HII region NGC 604.

Whirlpool Galaxy (M51)

Grand‑Design Spiral • Interacting Pair
Famous for its clean spiral arms enhanced by interaction with its companion galaxy.
  • About 23 million light‑years away in Canes Venatici.
  • Companion NGC 5195 distorts and sharpens its arms.

Sombrero Galaxy (M104)

Edge‑On View • Prominent Dust Lane
Known for its bright central bulge and a dramatic dust lane giving it a hat‑like appearance.
  • ~29 million light‑years away in Virgo.
  • Best seen in medium to large telescopes under dark skies.

What is a Nebula?

In Short

Nebulae are vast clouds of gas and dust in space. Some are stellar nurseries where stars are born; others are the glowing remains of dying stars.

Types

  • Emission: glow red from ionized hydrogen (H‑alpha).
  • Reflection: appear blue from scattered starlight.
  • Dark: dense dust blocks background light.
  • Planetary: shells from Sun‑like dying stars.
  • Supernova remnants: expanding debris from stellar explosions.

Scale, Color & Observing

  • Scale: can span many light‑years (Orion ~24 ly wide).
  • Why colorful? Long exposures map faint light; colors often represent different gases.
  • Observation tips: seek dark skies; use UHC/OIII filters for emission nebulae.

How to Observe

Use dark skies and a star chart or app. A small telescope reveals more structure; long-exposure images show even more detail.

Famous Nebulae

Spectacular clouds of gas and dust — stellar birthplaces and stellar remains.

Orion (M42) Eagle (M16) Crab (M1) Ring (M57) Carina

Orion Nebula (M42)

Star Nursery • Bright Emission Nebula
A nearby stellar nursery where new stars form within glowing hydrogen clouds.
  • ~1,300 light‑years away; visible to the naked eye in Orion’s sword.
  • Home to the young Trapezium star cluster.

Eagle Nebula (M16)

Pillars of Creation • Star‑Forming
Famous for towering columns of gas where stars are actively forming.
  • ~7,000 light‑years away in the constellation Serpens.
  • Made iconic by Hubble’s “Pillars of Creation” image.

Crab Nebula (M1)

Supernova Remnant • Pulsar
The expanding remains of a star that exploded in 1054 CE, with a rapidly spinning neutron star at its heart.
  • ~6,500 light‑years away in Taurus.
  • Emits across radio to gamma‑ray wavelengths.

Ring Nebula (M57)

Planetary Nebula • Dying Sun‑like Star
A glowing shell of gas ejected by a Sun‑like star at the end of its life, appearing as a bright ring.
  • ~2,300 light‑years away in Lyra.
  • Compact and bright — a good target for small telescopes.

Carina Nebula

Massive Complex • Southern Sky
A colossal region with dramatic pillars and massive stars shaping it, including the unstable Eta Carinae.
  • ~7,500 light‑years away in Carina (best from southern latitudes).
  • Home to clusters Trumpler 14 and 16.
