Solar System Planets

Quick facts about the eight planets orbiting our Sun.

Mercury Venus Earth Mars Jupiter Saturn Uranus Neptune

Mercury

Smallest • Innermost
The closest planet to the Sun with extreme temperature swings and a cratered, Moon-like surface.
  • Average distance from Sun: ~58 million km
  • Day length: 59 Earth days • Year length: 88 Earth days

Venus

Earth's Twin • Runaway Greenhouse
Similar in size to Earth but with a thick, toxic atmosphere and surface temperatures hot enough to melt lead.
  • Thick CO₂ atmosphere and sulfuric acid clouds
  • Retrograde rotation (spins opposite most planets)

Earth

Our Home • Liquid Water
The only known world to harbor life, with a protective atmosphere and abundant liquid water.
  • 1 Moon • Diverse climates and biosphere
  • Atmosphere: Nitrogen-Oxygen dominated

Mars

The Red Planet
A cold desert world with the largest volcano and canyon in the solar system and evidence of past water.
  • Moons: Phobos & Deimos
  • Explored by multiple rovers and orbiters

Jupiter

Gas Giant • Great Red Spot
The largest planet with powerful storms and dozens of moons including the volcanic Io and icy Europa.
  • Diameter: ~143,000 km • Strong magnetic field
  • At least 95 moons confirmed

Saturn

Iconic Rings • Gas Giant
Known for its stunning ring system; home to Titan, a moon with thick atmosphere and liquid methane lakes.
  • Rings: Ice and rock particles
  • Moons: 146 confirmed (incl. Titan & Enceladus)

Uranus

Ice Giant • Tilted Axis
An ice giant that rotates on its side, likely due to a massive ancient collision, with a pale blue hue from methane.
  • Axial tilt ~98° (spins on its side)
  • Faint rings and many moons

Neptune

Ice Giant • Supersonic Winds
The most distant known planet; deep blue with extreme winds and the large moon Triton.
  • Orbital period: 165 Earth years
  • Discovered in 1846 via mathematical prediction

Famous Stars

Iconic stars you may have heard of — explained simply.

Sun Sirius Betelgeuse Rigel Polaris Vega

Sun

Our Star • Life-Giver
The Sun is a medium-sized star that powers life on Earth by constantly fusing hydrogen into helium.
  • It’s 1.3 million Earths in volume — yet an average star by cosmic standards.
  • Sunlight you feel today left the Sun about 8 minutes ago.

Sirius

Dog Star • Brightest in Night Sky
The brightest star we see from Earth (after the Sun). It’s actually a pair: Sirius A and a faint white dwarf, Sirius B.
  • About 8.6 light-years away — relatively close neighbors.
  • So bright because it’s both luminous and nearby.

Betelgeuse

Red Supergiant • Shoulder of Orion
A colossal, cool star nearing the end of its life. It’s so big that if it replaced our Sun, it could stretch past Jupiter.
  • Known for its orangey-red glow in the Orion constellation.
  • Will one day explode as a supernova — spectacular, but not dangerous to us.

Rigel

Blue Supergiant • Foot of Orion
A hot, very luminous blue star. Though much farther than Betelgeuse, it still looks bright because it shines intensely.
  • Type: Blue supergiant — much hotter than our Sun.
  • Helps outline the famous Orion constellation with Betelgeuse.

Polaris

North Star • Sky Compass
Located almost directly above Earth’s North Pole, it appears fixed in the sky, helping navigators find north.
  • Not the brightest star, but very useful for direction.
  • Actually a small system of multiple stars.

Vega

Summer Star • Calibration Standard
A bright blue-white star in the Lyra constellation; historically used as a standard for star brightness.
  • Part of the Summer Triangle with Altair and Deneb.
  • Only ~25 light-years away — a nearby, photogenic neighbor.
